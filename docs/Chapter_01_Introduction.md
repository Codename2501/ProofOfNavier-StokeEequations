# Chapter 01 — Introduction

## 1.1. The Navier–Stokes Global Regularity Problem

The three–dimensional incompressible Navier–Stokes equations describe the evolution of a velocity field $v(x,t)$ and its vorticity $\omega = \nabla \times v$:

$$\partial_t v + (v\cdot\nabla)v = -\nabla p + \nu \Delta v, \qquad \nabla\cdot v = 0.$$

The question of whether smooth initial data can lead to a finite–time singularity remains one of the most fundamental open problems in mathematics.  
The difficulty is concentrated in the **vorticity stretching term**

$$(\omega\cdot\nabla)v,$$

which can amplify $\|\omega\|$ and potentially drive blow–up.

Classical analysis treats the equations within a fixed geometric background (Euclidean metric, standard Laplacian).  
This work introduces a **generative geometric framework** in which the geometry itself is produced from the vorticity field.

## 1.3. Vorticity, Stretching, and Anisotropic Dissipation

The central idea is that vorticity generates **directional structure**. This structure induces:

- an **OS-metric** $g_{ij}$ aligned with vorticity,
- an **OS-connection** $\Gamma^{i}{}_{jk}$,
- an **OS-Laplacian** $\Delta_{\mathrm{OS}}$,

which together create an **anisotropic dissipation mechanism**.

The OS-Laplacian is not isotropic like the classical Laplacian;  
it diffuses preferentially along dynamically generated directions, counteracting vorticity stretching.

This leads to a geometric damping effect (“OS-Damper”) that does not appear in the classical formulation.

## 1.4. Main Idea: OS-Generated Geometry

The Navier–Stokes equations are rewritten in the generative geometry:

$$\partial_t \omega = -(v\cdot\nabla)\omega + (\omega\cdot\nabla)v + \nu \Delta_{\mathrm{OS}} \omega.$$

This equation, called **OSG2ADV**, is *exactly equivalent* to the classical vorticity equation, but expressed in a geometry generated by $\omega$ itself.

The OS-metric introduces a vorticity–dependent distortion:

$$g^{ij} = \delta^{ij} + \alpha \|\omega\|^2 \hat{\omega}^i \hat{\omega}^j,$$

which enhances dissipation along the stretching direction.
